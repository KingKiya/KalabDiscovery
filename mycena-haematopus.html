Mycena haematopus

Mycena haematopus (commonly known as the bleeding fairy-helmet) is a species of mushroom native to the temperate and subtropical regions of the Northern hemisphere. It is a medium-sized mushroom, usually found in the woods on dead trees.

The cap of Mycena haematopus is cream to white in color and convex with a central knob. It typically measures between one and three centimeters in diameter and is often covered with dark smudges or spots. When fresh, the caps of Mycena haematopus also exude a red fluid which looks like droplets of blood. This is why the mushroom is named “bleeding fairy-helmet.”

The gills are cream-colored and adnexed or adnate. The stem is thick and white, usually between two and five centimeters long and between two and five millimeters thick. It is often bent at the base.

Mycena haematopus is edible and can be collected for culinary use. However, it should be noted that some people have an allergic reaction to this species when eaten raw, resulting in stomach pain, nausea, and vomiting. It is generally best to cook it first before consuming.

The ideal environment for Mycena haematopus is wet, dead wood, and the mushroom can be found growing in short clusters or solitary specimens. It grows in the temperate forest year round, and can be found in many areas of North America, Europe and Asia.

Mycena haematopus is of little interest to professional mycologists for research purposes. It is frequently mentioned as a curiosity when discussing forest mushrooms. Its colorful appearance and the unique fluid it exudes when fresh make it a good identification species for beginning mushroom collectors.
